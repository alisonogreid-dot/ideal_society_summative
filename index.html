This page is your home base for the research paper. Use it to find sources, choose your angle, and structure your writing.

Your Research Paper in One Sentence

You are writing a research paper that argues which factors matter most for creating an ideal society. You'll pick a priority — a lens through which you examine the factors we've studied — and use evidence to support your argument.

The six priorities below are starting points. You can also propose your own direction — check with your teacher first.

Six Priorities

Each priority leads to a different argument about what an ideal society needs most. Read through them and think about which one connects to the factors that interest you.

Equal Outcomes

What if the priority is making sure no one is left behind — that wealth, health, and opportunity are distributed fairly?

Connects to: health, education, standard of living, equity

Individual Freedoms

What if the most important thing is that people are free to speak, know, and take part in public life?

Connects to: free press, transparency, participation

Citizen Power

What if the priority is that citizens can hold power to account and create change — even when the system resists?

Connects to: agency, accountability, participation

Stability & Security

What if the most important thing is that the society is safe, predictable, and well-governed — even if that means some trade-offs?

Connects to: security, fairness, accountability

Living with Difference

What if a society's strength comes from holding many ways of living — rather than demanding conformity?

Connects to: tolerance (draws on Popper's paradox, secularism, More's Utopia)

Long-Term Survival

What if we focus on whether the society can sustain itself — environmentally, economically, and socially — for generations?

Connects to: sustainability, innovation

Don't see yourself in any of these six? That's fine. Some students might want to argue for a blend, or take a completely different angle — for example, focusing on cultural identity, technological access, or community belonging. Talk to your teacher about your idea before you start researching.

From Priority to Research Question

Your research question should connect your chosen priority to the factors from the unit. A good research question is specific enough to answer in 2-3 pages but open enough that you need evidence to answer it.

Example Research Questions

"To what extent are individual freedoms the most important factor in creating an ideal society?"

"Is long-term environmental sustainability more important than economic growth when building a society people want to live in?"

"How far does political stability contribute to human development, and what does it cost?"

Notice: each question names a priority, connects it to factors, and can be argued from multiple sides.

Your Paper Structure

A social science research paper separates what the evidence says from what you think it means. This is different from an essay where you argue throughout. Here, you present first, then discuss.

Click each section below to see what goes in it and example sentence starters.

What goes here: Introduce your research question. Explain what priority you've chosen and why it matters. Give the reader a brief overview of what you'll cover.

Keep it short — 4-6 sentences. Don't start arguing yet.

Sentence Starters

"This paper investigates whether [priority] is the most important factor in creating an ideal society."

"Throughout this unit, we have explored factors including [name 2-3]. This paper focuses on [your priority] because..."

"The question of what makes a society ideal has no single answer. However, by examining [your priority], it is possible to argue that..."

What goes here: Present the data and information you found. Report what the evidence says — don't argue or give your opinion yet. Organise this section by factor or by theme.

This is the longest section. You might have 2-3 sub-sections, each covering a different factor or piece of evidence.

Include: numbers, statistics, rankings, examples from specific countries, and references to your sources.

Presenting Data

"According to [source], [country] scores [number] on the [index/measure]."

"The data from [source] shows that countries with higher levels of [factor] tend to..."

"[Country] provides an example of this pattern. Its [factor score] is [number], while its [other measure] is [number]."

Presenting Examples

"One country that illustrates this is [country], where..."

"A contrasting example can be seen in [country], which has [high/low factor] but [different outcome]."

In-text citations: Every time you use data or an idea from a source, say where it came from. Use the format: (Source Name, Year) — for example, (Freedom House, 2024) or (UNDP, 2025).

What goes here: Now you interpret the evidence. What does it mean? Make your argument: which factors matter most and why? This is where your critical thinking lives.

This section must also include another perspective — someone who chose a different priority would argue differently. Acknowledge that and explain why your argument is still convincing.

Making Your Argument

"The evidence suggests that [factor] is essential to an ideal society because..."

"When comparing [Country A] and [Country B], it becomes clear that [factor] makes a significant difference because..."

"This data supports the idea that [your priority] matters most, since..."

Other Perspectives

"However, someone who prioritises [other priority] might argue that..."

"A limitation of focusing on [your priority] is that it may overlook..."

"While [factor] is important, [country example] achieved [positive outcome] through a different approach, focusing on..."

Connecting to Concepts

"This connects to the key concept of systems because..."

"This links to the related concept of equity because..."

"The trade-off between [factor A] and [factor B] reflects how systems must balance competing needs."

What goes here: Answer your research question directly. Summarise your main argument in 2-3 sentences. No new evidence here — just wrap up clearly.

Sentence Starters

"In conclusion, this paper has argued that [priority] is the most important factor because..."

"While no society is perfect, the evidence shows that societies which prioritise [factor] tend to..."

"Ultimately, an ideal society requires [main point], though it must also consider [trade-off]."

What goes here: A list of every source you used, in alphabetical order by organisation or author name.

Reference Format

Organisation Name. (Year). "Page Title." Website Name. URL

Examples:

Freedom House. (2024). "Freedom in the World 2024." Freedom House. https://freedomhouse.org/countries/freedom-world/scores

UNDP. (2025). "Human Development Index." Human Development Reports. https://hdr.undp.org/data-center/human-development-index

The key difference between Evidence and Discussion: In your Evidence section, you're a reporter — just present what you found. In your Discussion, you're an analyst — explain what it means, why it matters, and what someone else might think.

Your Two-Week Plan

You have one week for research and one week for writing. Here's how to use your time.

Week 1 — Research

Single Lesson

Choose your angle and draft your research question. Browse the Source Library and identify 3-5 sources you plan to use. Begin reading and taking notes.

Double Lesson

Deep research. Read your sources carefully. Record key data, statistics, and examples in your research document. Make sure you have evidence for at least 2-3 factors. Note your sources for the reference list.

Week 2 — Writing

Single Lesson

Write your Introduction and start Evidence. Use the Writing Guide tab. Get your research question and key findings onto paper.

Double Lesson

Finish Evidence, write Discussion and Conclusion. Add references. Proofread. Check the success criteria below before submitting.

Success Criteria

Check these off before you submit.

Criterion B — Investigating

Your paper has a clear, focused research question (B.i)
You used at least 3 sources and recorded where your information came from (B.iii)
You include in-text citations — (Source, Year) — whenever you use data or ideas from a source (B.iii)
Your reference list is complete and matches the sources you cited (B.iii)

Criterion D — Thinking Critically

You analyse factors from the unit — not just describe them — and connect them to your argument (D.i)
Your Discussion makes a clear argument supported by evidence from the Evidence section (D.ii)
You acknowledge at least one other perspective or priority and explain the trade-offs (D.iv)
You use the key concept (systems) and the related concept (equity) from the unit (D.i)

General

Your Evidence and Discussion sections are clearly separated
You use key terms from the unit accurately (HDI, equity, participation, accountability, agency, etc.)
Your conclusion answers your research question — it connects back to the introduction
